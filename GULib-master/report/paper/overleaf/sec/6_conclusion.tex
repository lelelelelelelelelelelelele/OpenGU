\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\paragraph{Summary.}
We presented the first systematic adversarial audit of approximate graph
unlearning. By treating deletion-set selection as the attacker's primary
degree of freedom, we exposed a sharp \emph{vulnerability spectrum}
governed by the unlearning algorithm's mechanism family. Learning-based
GNNDelete collapses under tiny deletion budgets; influence-function GIF
shows a small, Newton-step-bounded effect; shard-based GraphEraser
exhibits a counterintuitive \emph{Shard Protection Effect}; and
feature-preserving / certified families resist all six selectors we
deployed.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(i)~Our selectors target gradient or diffusion semantics; methods whose
unlearning operator does not surface those signals (MEGU, IDEA) appear
robust, but we frame this as a \emph{limitation of the current attacker
class} rather than a security guarantee. A natural next step is to design
selectors that exploit the specific algebraic structure of certified or
mutual-information-based unlearners.
(ii)~We focus on node-classification with the small-budget regime
$r\le0.05$; behavior at large budgets, on edge/feature unlearning, or on
heterogeneous graphs is left for future work.
(iii)~Our experiments cover three datasets and two backbones; large-scale
industrial deployments may exhibit additional phenomena (e.g., shard count
sensitivity) that single-graph benchmarks cannot surface.

\paragraph{Broader implications.}
Our results argue that any future approximate GU method should report not
only retrain-time speedup and random-deletion utility but also a
worst-case adversarial-deletion bound. The diagnostic kit we
introduce---paired effect, retrain gap, prediction shift, hop-distance
decay---is independent of our specific selectors and can be reused with
arbitrary attacks. We believe this aligns the GU community with the
robustness-evaluation norms of adversarial machine learning more broadly.

\paragraph{Future directions.}
Three open problems stand out.
(1)~\emph{Mechanism-aware selectors:} a selector that targets MEGU's
MI-regularizer or IDEA's certification would test whether the
mechanism-incomparable robustness we observe is a true property or a
selector artifact.
(2)~\emph{Defenses:} the Shard Protection Effect suggests that
partition-based aggregation may be a principled defense; quantifying the
privacy-vs-robustness trade-off of shard-based GU is the natural
follow-up.
(3)~\emph{Beyond utility:} our attacks target downstream F1; analogous
attacks targeting privacy leakage (e.g., MIA AUC reduction \emph{after}
unlearning) would close the threat-model loop.
