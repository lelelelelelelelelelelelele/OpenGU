\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Machine unlearning is no longer optional. Privacy regulations~\cite{gdpr2016,ccpa2018} grant data subjects the
right to request removal of their data from trained models (GDPR
Art.\ 17, CCPA \S1798.105), and graph learning
systems---recommendation, fraud detection, citation analysis,
knowledge-graph-augmented retrieval---are squarely in scope. The naive
solution is retrain-from-scratch, but for production GNNs this is often
prohibitive. \emph{Approximate graph unlearning} (GU)
methods~\cite{chen2022graph,wu2023gif,cheng2023gnndelete,li2024towards,dong2024idea,zhang2024towards}
trade exact removal for large speedups via gradient-, influence-, or
partition-based updates.

This trade-off is justified under the implicit assumption that deletion
requests are \emph{benign}. In adversarial settings, this assumption 
critically fails: a coalition of users on a compliance-driven platform 
can selectively choose \emph{which} records to forget via legitimate APIs.

We reveal that this single degree of freedom---deletion-set selection---uncovers 
a deeply fragmented, yet highly predictable, security landscape in approximate GU. 
Rather than a random assortment of weaknesses, we expose a clear \textbf{Design-Vulnerability Pattern}: 
an algorithm's susceptibility is strictly governed by its underlying unlearning mechanics. 
On one extreme, \emph{Learning-based} methods (e.g., GNNDelete) suffer a domino-like utility 
collapse (dropping up to $24\%$ in absolute F1 on degree-based deletion), as their coupled loss objectives
fail to contain structural cascading errors. \emph{Influence-based} methods are precision-targeted 
by gradient-aligned attacks. On the other extreme, \emph{Partition-based} methods exhibit a natural 
physical isolation, demonstrating \emph{architectural immunity} (Shard Protection). 
This predictable vulnerability dictates that exposing a deletion API without understanding 
these underlying architectural flaws risks the API being \emph{weaponized} to silently dismantle model utility.

\paragraph{This paper.}
We conduct the first systematic adversarial audit of approximate GU
under a deletion-API threat model with a calibrated four-tier access
spectrum (L0 / L1-structural / L2-\textsc{direct} /
L2-\textsc{surrogate}; see Table~\ref{tab:access}), treating
deletion-set selection as the attack and post-unlearning F1 as victim
utility. To separate family-level trends from method-specific
behavior, we test \textbf{intra-family pairs} drawn from the upstream
OpenGU taxonomy. Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Threat model and selectors.} We formalize adversarial
        deletion attacks and organize six selectors along a calibrated
        four-tier access spectrum (L0 deletion-only / L1 structural
        / L2-\textsc{direct} / L2-\textsc{surrogate}) keyed on
        resource cost, not on assumed sophistication.
  \item \textbf{Vulnerability Fingerprint.} We project attack-specific F1
        effects onto structural-spread (IM) and gradient-signal (TracIn)
        axes across six GU methods, revealing family-level regimes and
        strong within-family outliers.
  \item \textbf{Alignment mechanism.} We surface a structural
        alignment regularity (\S\ref{sec:results-alignment}): mean
        degree of the selected set predicts paired F1 effect across
        $n{=}300$ tuples (Pearson $r{=}0.24$, $p{<}10^{-4}$) and
        within $11/12$ method$\times$backbone cells, framing the
        observed selector ordering as an objective-mismatch artefact
        and motivating attack-aware selectors as future work.
  \item \textbf{Collateral diagnostics.} We separate the $k{=}5$ noise
        floor, budget-matched random deletion, and attack-specific paired
        signal, and complement them with retrain gap, prediction shift,
        hop-distance decay, and update-detection AUC.
  \item \textbf{Defense narrative.} \emph{Shard Protection}---the
        architectural immunity of the partition pair to every selector
        and every budget tested---refines when partition-based GU
        functions as a defense, and the GraphRevoker$\times$GAT
        TracIn breakthrough delineates its boundary.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why this matters now.}
GU is moving toward deletion APIs just as approximate methods are being
benchmarked at ogbn scale. Existing evaluations mostly assume random
deletion, creating a false sense of security. Our audit introduces the 
missing worst-case view and a diagnostic protocol for reporting vulnerability 
alongside efficiency, fundamentally shifting the paradigm from ``how fast can we forget?'' to ``how safely can we expose the forget API?''
