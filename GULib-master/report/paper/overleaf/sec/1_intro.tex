\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Machine unlearning is no longer optional. Privacy regulations
(GDPR Art.\ 17, CCPA \S1798.105, EU AI Act) grant data subjects the right to
request removal of their data from trained models, and graph learning
systems---recommendation, fraud detection, citation analysis,
knowledge-graph-augmented retrieval---are squarely in scope.
The naive solution is retrain-from-scratch, but for production GNNs over
million-node graphs this is computationally prohibitive. \emph{Approximate
graph unlearning} (GU) methods~\cite{chen2022graph,wu2023gif,cheng2023gnndelete,li2024towards,dong2024idea,zhang2024towards}
trade exact removal for orders-of-magnitude speedup by performing
gradient-, influence-, or partition-based local updates.

This trade-off is justified under the implicit assumption that deletion
requests are \emph{benign}---i.e., independent of the model. In adversarial
settings this assumption fails. An adversary who controls even a small
fraction of deletion requests (e.g., a coalition of users on a platform that
honors deletion-by-API) can choose \emph{which} data to forcibly remove.
We show that this single degree of freedom---selecting the deletion set---is
sufficient to collapse the utility of widely-deployed approximate GU methods.

\paragraph{This paper.}
We conduct the first systematic adversarial audit of approximate GU, treating
deletion-set selection as the attacker's strategy and post-unlearning
downstream F1 as the victim's utility. Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Threat model and attack framework} (\S\ref{sec:method}).
        We formalize \emph{adversarial deletion attacks} on GU and instantiate
        six selection strategies, including a TracIn-based pseudo-influence
        score, a CELF-style influence-maximization (IM) strategy, and a hybrid
        IF+IM combination.
  \item \textbf{Cross-family vulnerability spectrum} (\S\ref{sec:results}).
        We benchmark five representative GU methods spanning three mechanism
        families on Cora, Citeseer, and ogbn-arxiv with GCN/GAT backbones, and
        show that vulnerability is governed by the \emph{algorithm family},
        not by any specific implementation choice.
  \item \textbf{Shard Protection Effect.} We document and explain a
        counterintuitive phenomenon: shard-based unlearning
        (GraphEraser~\cite{chen2022graph}) is not merely robust but
        \emph{benefits} from deletion---random and adversarial deletion both
        improve downstream F1, with the attacker's gain bounded above by
        random selection.
  \item \textbf{Collateral damage diagnostics.} We introduce three metrics
        that decouple targeted forgetting from collateral disturbance:
        retrain gap, prediction shift, and a novel \emph{hop-distance decay}
        statistic that traces how unlearning propagates through the GNN's
        receptive field.
  \item \textbf{Mechanism-incomparable robustness.} We identify two GU
        families (feature-preserving MEGU~\cite{li2024towards}, certified
        IDEA~\cite{dong2024idea}) on which all six attack strategies---
        including model-coupled influence functions---fail to produce a
        meaningful effect, and explain why current selectors cannot exploit
        them.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why this matters now.}
Three factors converge. First, GU is moving from research code to production:
the past two years have seen at least five competitive approximate methods
benchmarked on
ogbn-scale~\cite{wu2023gif,cheng2023gnndelete,dong2024idea,li2024towards,zhang2024towards}.
Second, regulators are mandating deletion APIs at the same time. Third, the
GNN community has not yet stress-tested these methods against
\emph{adversarial} deletion---only random or uniform settings. Our work
closes that gap and provides a principled diagnostic kit
(\S\ref{sec:method}) that any future GU paper can use to report vulnerability
upper-bounds alongside efficiency.
