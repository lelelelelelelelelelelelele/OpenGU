\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Machine unlearning is no longer optional. Privacy regulations
(GDPR Art.\ 17, CCPA \S1798.105, EU AI Act) grant data subjects the right
to request removal of their data from trained models, and graph learning
systems---recommendation, fraud detection, citation analysis,
knowledge-graph-augmented retrieval---are squarely in scope. The naive
solution is retrain-from-scratch, but for production GNNs this is often
prohibitive. \emph{Approximate graph unlearning} (GU)
methods~\cite{chen2022graph,wu2023gif,cheng2023gnndelete,li2024towards,dong2024idea,zhang2024towards}
trade exact removal for large speedups via gradient-, influence-, or
partition-based updates.

This trade-off is justified under the implicit assumption that deletion
requests are \emph{benign}. In adversarial settings this assumption fails:
a coalition of users on a deletion-by-API platform can choose
\emph{which} records to forget. We show that this single degree of
freedom---deletion-set selection---can collapse some approximate GU
methods while leaving others, even within the same OpenGU families,
effectively untouched.

\paragraph{This paper.}
We conduct the first systematic adversarial audit of approximate GU,
treating deletion-set selection as the attack and post-unlearning F1 as
victim utility. To separate family-level trends from method-specific
behavior, we test \textbf{intra-family pairs} drawn from the upstream
OpenGU taxonomy. Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Threat model and selectors.} We formalize adversarial
        deletion attacks and organize six selectors by access cost:
        deletion-only, structural, model-coupled, and surrogate-model
        tiers.
  \item \textbf{Vulnerability Fingerprint.} We project attack-specific F1
        effects onto structural-spread (IM) and gradient-signal (TracIn)
        axes across six GU methods, revealing family-level regimes and
        strong within-family outliers.
  \item \textbf{Collateral diagnostics.} We separate architectural F1
        shift from attack-specific signal via $\Delta F_{\text{total}} =
        \Delta F_{\text{arch}}+\Delta F_{\text{attack}}$, and complement
        it with retrain gap, prediction shift, hop-distance decay, and MIA.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why this matters now.}
GU is moving toward deletion APIs just as approximate methods are being
benchmarked at ogbn scale. Existing evaluations mostly assume random
deletion. Our audit adds the missing worst-case deletion-set view and a
diagnostic protocol for reporting vulnerability alongside efficiency.
