Table~\ref{tab:master} summarizes our headline finding: the dominant
predictor of vulnerability is the \emph{mechanism family}, not the dataset,
the backbone, or the choice of selection strategy. The five-method ordering
in Panel~A reads as a vulnerability spectrum---GNNDelete (Learn) suffers
the largest drop, GIF (IF) shows a small but bounded effect, GraphEraser
(Shard) trends \emph{negative} (the Shard Protection Effect of
\S\ref{sec:results-shard}), and feature-preserving / certified families
(MEGU, IDEA) remain near zero. Panel~B replicates the same family ordering
on a 60$\times$ larger graph (ogbn-arxiv, 169K nodes), restricted to
three family representatives and four selectors due to the GPU budget;
the full 5-family $\times$ 6-strategy matrix on cora/GAT and the
ogbn-arxiv feasibility analysis appear in Appendix~\ref{app:results}. We
organize the remainder of \S\ref{sec:results} around this spectrum.

\subsection{GNNDelete: Severe Vulnerability Under Tiny Budgets}
\label{sec:results-gnndelete}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/FIG-1_Generalization.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{GNNDelete generalization across deletion ratios}
  (Cora/GCN, single seed). F1 drop grows monotonically with the
  deletion budget for IM and Hybrid strategies and remains substantial
  even at $r=0.01$ (27 nodes), suggesting that the vulnerability is not
  a budget-dependent artifact. \interim{Update with N=5 error bars.}}
  \label{fig:gnndelete-ratio}
\end{figure}

GNNDelete is the only method on which all model-coupled attack strategies
(TracIn, IM, Hybrid) produce a large and statistically meaningful F1 drop.
On Cora/GCN at $r=0.05$, the IM-selected deletion set drives F1 down by
\interim{16.9\%} on average across 5 seeds (paired vs.\ random,
$p$\interim{$<$0.001}, 95\%~CI \interim{[14.0, 19.7]}).
Figure~\ref{fig:gnndelete-ratio} traces the F1 drop across deletion ratios
$r\in\{0.01,0.05,0.10,0.20\}$: the effect remains substantial even at
$r=0.01$ (27 nodes out of $\sim$2700), ruling out a "budget-saturation"
explanation.

\paragraph{Why is GNNDelete vulnerable?}
GNNDelete optimizes a deletion-aware objective that explicitly targets the
deleted set's representations to match a no-information prior. When the
attacker selects nodes that are central in the test-set's receptive field,
this targeted erasure creates large representation gaps that propagate
through downstream message-passing. Section~\ref{sec:results-collateral}
quantifies this propagation.

\subsection{GIF: Bounded by the Newton Step}
\label{sec:results-gif}

GIF's vulnerability is consistent but small: \interim{$+4.17\%$} F1
effect for TracIn on Cora/GCN ($N=5$, $p\interim{=0.0001}$). Hybrid and
IM produce similar magnitudes within statistical noise. We attribute this
ceiling to the \emph{single-Newton-step} update GIF performs: the inverse
Hessian regularization absorbs most of the deletion shock and the
remaining error is approximately linear in the deletion-set's local
curvature. The attacker can sharpen this curvature by choosing
high-influence training nodes (TracIn does this directly), but cannot
break out of the Newton-step bound.

\subsection{The Shard Protection Effect}
\label{sec:results-shard}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/FIG-2_Scaling.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Shard Protection Effect} (GraphEraser, Cora/GCN).
  Across all six selection strategies, F1 \emph{increases} relative to
  the no-deletion baseline. The attacker's gain is bounded above by
  random selection. \interim{Refresh with Phase B numbers.}}
  \label{fig:shard}
\end{figure}

Shard-based unlearning produces a counterintuitive result: every selection
strategy yields a \emph{negative} attack effect on F1
(Figure~\ref{fig:shard}). On Cora/GCN, random deletion improves F1 by
\interim{6.27\%} and adversarial selectors improve it by
\interim{3-6\%}. We call this the \textbf{Shard Protection Effect}.

\paragraph{Mechanism.}
GraphEraser partitions the graph into $C$ disjoint shards, trains a
sub-classifier per shard, and aggregates by learnable attention. Deleting a
training node only affects the shard it belongs to; the aggregator's other
shards are untouched. Empirically, deletion of high-degree (heuristic) or
high-influence (TracIn/IM) nodes \emph{purifies} a shard---removing nodes
that span class boundaries leads to cleaner sub-classifiers, and the
attention aggregator is robust enough to absorb the change. The attacker's
upper bound is therefore set by random selection, which uniformly thins
each shard.

\paragraph{Implication.}
For shard-based GU, adversarial deletion is \emph{not} a meaningful
threat---and may even be a useful regularizer. We do not interpret this
as a recommendation to deploy GraphEraser unconditionally; the trade-off
sits in privacy guarantees rather than utility.

\subsection{MEGU and IDEA: Mechanism-Incomparable Robustness}
\label{sec:results-mechanism}

MEGU (feature-preserving with mutual-information constraint) and IDEA
(certified gradient with statistical guarantee) form a third regime: every
attack strategy we instantiate produces $<\interim{1\%}$ F1 effect, with
$p$-values consistent with the null. We considered two explanations:
(a)~\emph{true robustness}, where the algorithms genuinely localize damage;
(b)~\emph{mechanism incomparability}, where our selectors (which assume
gradient/influence/diffusion semantics) cannot reach the algorithms'
attack surfaces because their unlearning operators do not honor those
semantics in the same way. We lean toward (b) as the dominant explanation:
neither MEGU's MI-regularizer nor IDEA's certified projection has a
gradient signal that TracIn or IM can target. We frame this as a
\emph{limitation of the current attacker class} rather than a security
claim, and discuss future selector designs in \S\ref{sec:conclusion}.

\label{sec:results-collateral}

  \label{fig:significance}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Significance.}
Figure~\ref{fig:significance} reports the paired $t$-test over five seeds
for every (method, strategy) cell. With the new \texttt{im\_selector\_seed}
fix (\S\ref{sec:strategies}), all but one of the GNNDelete/IM/Hybrid cells
clear $p<0.05$.\footnote{In our pre-fix data the GNNDelete/IM cell had
$p=0.076$; we attribute this to selector-induced variance with
Jaccard\,$=$\,0.13 across seeds. After fixing the IM Monte-Carlo seed the
selector is deterministic across GNN seeds.} The GIF row clears
$p<0.001$ for TracIn but Hybrid is borderline, consistent with our
"Newton-step ceiling" interpretation in \S\ref{sec:results-gif}.

\paragraph{Retrain gap.}
Across methods, the F1 drop is \emph{not} explained by simply having
removed informative nodes: retraining from scratch on
$\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{tr}}\setminus S$ recovers baseline-level F1
(retrain gap \interim{$<$2\%} for GIF and shard-based methods). For
GNNDelete the retrain gap is \interim{20.5\%} on average, confirming
that the F1 collapse comes from the unlearning algorithm's approximation
error rather than from data loss.

\paragraph{Hop-distance decay.}
Binning test nodes by graph distance to the nearest deleted node, GIF and
shard-based methods exhibit a sharp decay (most damage in the 1-hop
bucket, near-zero in the 3+ bucket). GNNDelete shows nearly uniform
F1 degradation across all four buckets, indicating that its
representation-level objective propagates the deletion globally rather
than localizing it. \interim{Insert hop-decay figure once Phase B
results land; per-family decay curves go in Appendix.}
