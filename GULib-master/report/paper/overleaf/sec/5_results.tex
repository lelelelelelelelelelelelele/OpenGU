\paragraph{Reading guide.}
Table~\ref{tab:benchmark} consolidates the audit's main result across
both backbones. Five patterns are visible at a glance: \emph{(i)} the
partition family is attack-immune --- no strategy clears $p{<}.05$
on five of six (method, backbone) cells, with the lone exception
GraphRevoker$\times$GAT (TracIn / Hybrid breakthrough). \emph{(ii)}
\emph{Best attack} is \textsc{Degree} in 8 of 12 cells and never
\textsc{TracIn} on the non-partition methods, inverting the
conventional access-tier hierarchy. \emph{(iii)} The Verdict column
splits cleanly along family lines: \emph{Immune} for partition
(except one Conditional cell), \emph{Structural} or \emph{Mild} for
IF-based, and an asymmetric pair within Learning---GNNDelete posts
the largest mean magnitudes ($+6.0\%$/$+11.0\%$ retrain gap) but
$\sigma{=}7$--$10\%$ prevents conventional significance, while MEGU
clears $p{<}.001$ on four of five attack columns yet stays
small-scale. \emph{(iv)} GNNDelete is the unique
approximation-error outlier, with retrain gap an order of magnitude
larger than every other method (${\approx}11\%$ vs.\ ${<}2\%$).
\emph{(v)} Update AUC and F1 attack effect decouple: MEGU and IDEA
sit near AUC$\,{=}\,0.50$ regardless of attack scale, while
GraphRevoker reaches AUC${\,=\,}0.81$ despite immune utility. The
remaining subsections diagnose each pattern separately.

\subsection{Vulnerability Fingerprint}
\label{sec:results-fingerprint}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{figures/FIG-3_Spectrum.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Vulnerability Fingerprint} on the canonical cell
  (Cora/GCN/$r{=}0.05$, 5 seeds). Each point is a method-level cluster
  centre with a 1$\sigma$ ellipse over seeds; axes are the
  attack-specific F1 effects $\Delta F^{\text{attack}}_{\mathrm{IM}}$
  (structural-spread, $\alpha{=}0$) and
  $\Delta F^{\text{attack}}_{\mathrm{TracIn}}$ (gradient-signal,
  $\alpha{=}1$). Marker shape distinguishes pair members within each
  OpenGU category; chords visualise within-pair distance.}
  \label{fig:fingerprint}
\end{figure}

The fingerprint plots the paired attack effect against the same-seed
random baseline, so its axes separate attack-specific signal from the
architectural term reported in \S\ref{sec:results-shard}. Because IM is
an L1 structural selector and TracIn is an L2-\textsc{direct}
model-coupled selector (Table~\ref{tab:access}), the plot also gives a
threat summary: the IM coordinate is a low-access floor, while the
TracIn coordinate is an upper bound on surrogate-model attacks.

Figure~\ref{fig:fingerprint} shows three regimes. \textbf{GNNDelete}
posts the largest mean IM-axis effect
($\Delta F^{\mathrm{attack}}_{\mathrm{IM}}{=}{+}3.2\%$,
$\Delta F^{\mathrm{attack}}_{\mathrm{TracIn}}{=}{-}4.6\%$;
$N{=}5$, $p_{\mathrm{IM}}{=}0.21$) but with high seed variance
(${\pm}8.0\%$ on IM), reflecting an unstable collapse mode rather than
a tightly localised one; the family-level signature still shows the
largest absolute F1 drop (mean ${\sim}13\%$, peak $23.6\%$). \textbf{GIF}
is smaller and \emph{degree-aligned} rather than TracIn-aligned
(degree-paired ${+}2.14\pm 0.21\%$, $p<10^{-4}$; TracIn-paired
${-}0.37\pm 0.60\%$, n.s.), consistent with structural-redundancy
amplification rather than a pure Newton-step signal.
\textbf{GraphEraser} and \textbf{GraphRevoker} remain near the origin
with attack coordinates within $\pm 1.5\%$, with one
backbone-conditional anomaly: GraphRevoker$\times$GAT$\times$TracIn
yields a significant paired effect ($+3.58\%$, $p{=}0.018$), the only
cell where TracIn measurably exceeds the random baseline.
\textbf{MEGU} and \textbf{IDEA} likewise stay near the origin
($<1.5\%$ on the IM and TracIn axes) despite belonging to ostensibly
vulnerable parent families. This reveals a distinct \textbf{Family
Pattern}: Learning-based methods are susceptible to severe, systemic
global collapse (albeit with strong internal heterogeneity); IF-based
methods (like GIF) are most vulnerable when attacked directly on their
native gradient-signal axis; and Partition-based methods rely on
physical sub-graph isolation for natural immunity against global
interference (yielding Shard Protection). A 1D projection is provided
in Appendix~\ref{app:results-1d}.

\subsection{Structural Alignment Predicts Attack Success}
\label{sec:results-alignment}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{figures/FIG-5_Alignment.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Structural alignment of a selector predicts its
  attack effect.} (a) Each translucent dot is one
  (method, strategy, backbone, seed) tuple ($n{=}300$, excluding
  random); opaque markers with error bars show strategy means.
  Selectors order along $\bar{d}$, the mean degree of selected nodes;
  strategies that pick higher-degree nodes deliver larger paired
  effects. (b) Strategy-level mean paired effects, annotated with
  $\bar{d}$. \textsc{TracIn} picks nodes of degree comparable to
  \textsc{Random} ($\bar{d}\!\approx\!3.9$, vs.\ graph mean $3.9$) and
  yields near-zero attack effect.}
  \label{fig:alignment}
\end{figure}

The fingerprint pattern in \S\ref{sec:results-fingerprint} raises an
immediate question: \emph{why} do gradient- and propagation-based
selectors fail relative to the simplest structural baseline?
Figure~\ref{fig:alignment} provides empirical traction. For each
(method, strategy, backbone, seed) tuple we record $\bar{d}$, the mean
degree of the selected nodes, and its same-seed paired F1 effect
against random.

\paragraph{Selection-degree predicts effect.}
Across $n{=}300$ non-random tuples, $\bar{d}$ correlates with the
paired F1 effect at Pearson $r{=}0.24$ (Spearman $\rho{=}0.34$,
$p{<}10^{-4}$). Strategy-level means are monotonic in $\bar{d}$:
\textsc{Degree} ($\bar{d}{=}18.7$, paired $+2.04\%$) $>$
\textsc{PageRank} ($18.5$, $+1.54\%$) $>$
\textsc{IM} ($13.1$, $+0.88\%$) $>$
\textsc{Hybrid} ($6.5$, $+0.42\%$) $>$
\textsc{TracIn} ($3.9$, $+0.02\%$). \textsc{TracIn}'s selected-node
degree is statistically indistinguishable from \textsc{Random}'s
($\bar{d}{=}3.94$ vs.\ $3.88$; graph mean is $3.90$): the gradient
signal produces selections of average degree, not high-leverage
structural targets.

\paragraph{Cell-level confirmation and the one exception.}
Within each (method, backbone) cell, the strategy-level correlation
between $\bar{d}$ and paired effect is positive in \textbf{11 of 12
cells}, with $r{>}0.9$ and $p{<}0.05$ on GIF$\times$GCN ($r{=}0.97$),
GNNDelete$\times$GCN ($0.99$), MEGU$\times$GCN ($0.94$), and
IDEA$\times$GAT ($0.97$). The lone negative-correlation cell---%
GraphRevoker$\times$GAT ($r{=}-0.78$)---is precisely the one cell
where TracIn yields a significant paired effect ($+3.58\%$,
$p{=}0.018$). The alignment hypothesis is internally consistent: it
predicts that sophisticated selectors win exactly when structural
alignment ceases to predict attack success.

\paragraph{Mechanism: objective misalignment.}
We frame this empirical regularity as an \emph{objective-misalignment
hypothesis}. TracIn optimizes per-sample influence on test loss;
IM-CELF optimizes propagation spread under independent-cascade
dynamics. Neither aligns with the true attack target---the
conditioning of the unlearning approximation operator $\mathcal{U}$
(Hessian conditioning for IF-based methods, mask-network optimization
stability for learning-based ones). The selectors that work do so by
accident: high-degree training nodes happen to maximally disrupt
$\mathcal{U}$'s implicit assumptions. We treat the
GraphRevoker$\times$GAT co-occurrence as a mechanism wedge rather
than a closed proof, and defer attack-aware selectors that directly
target $\mathcal{U}$'s approximation error to future work.

\subsection{The Shard Protection Effect}
\label{sec:results-shard}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/FIG-2_Scaling.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Architectural F1 shift $\Delta F_{\text{arch}}$ per
  method} (Cora/GCN, $r{=}0.05$). Bars below zero mark Shard
  Protection: the Partition pair's downstream F1 \emph{increases} when
  any small random set is unlearned. The fingerprint in
  \S\ref{sec:results-fingerprint} reports the orthogonal
  $\Delta F_{\text{attack}}$ term. Bars show mean over 5 seeds with
  $1\sigma$ error; budget-matched random ($r{=}0.05$).}
  \label{fig:shard-protection}
\end{figure}

For the Partition family, the architectural term is negative:
$\Delta F_{\text{arch}}<0$, meaning random deletion at $r=0.05$
\emph{improves} F1 by $5.8\%$ (GraphEraser, GCN) to $14.6\%$
(GraphRevoker, GAT). We call this \textbf{Shard Protection}. Crucially, it is not an attack effect:
GraphEraser and GraphRevoker have $\Delta F_{\text{attack}}\approx 0$
in Figure~\ref{fig:fingerprint}, so adversarial selectors do not amplify
the gain beyond the random-deletion baseline.

The mechanism hypothesis is that partition aggregation localizes
unlearning to the affected shard while the remaining shard ensemble
absorbs the perturbation. We leave shard-purity and shard-count evidence
to Appendix~\ref{app:shard}; the main-text claim is the decomposition:
Shard Protection belongs to $\Delta F_{\text{arch}}$, not to the
attacker.

\subsection{Collateral Damage and Hop-Distance Decay}
\label{sec:results-hop}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{figures/FIG-4a_Significance.pdf}
  \hfill
  \includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{figures/FIG-4b_Effect.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Effect-size and significance heatmap} (5-seed paired
  $t$-test, Cora/GCN, $r{=}0.05$). Each cell in the left panel is
  colored by the $p$-value of the one-sided test $\mu\le 0$; right
  panel reports the paired mean effect size in F1 percentage points.
  Selector seeds are decoupled from training seeds (Jaccard${=}1.0$
  across the IM/Hybrid runs; see Appendix~\ref{app:imseed}).}
  \label{fig:significance}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:significance} tests the paired effect over seeds.
With the \texttt{im\_selector\_seed} fix (\S\ref{app:imseed}),
GNNDelete shows large mean IM/Hybrid effects but high seed variance
that prevents conventional significance ($p_{\mathrm{IM}}{=}0.21$,
$p_{\mathrm{Hybrid}}{=}0.76$ on Cora/GCN); GIF, IDEA, and MEGU each
clear $p<0.01$ on degree and pagerank but not on TracIn; the
Partition pair clears no attack strategy at $p<0.05$, consistent
with their architectural immunity.

The retrain gap verifies that utility loss is not merely data loss:
retraining on $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{tr}}\setminus S$ recovers
baseline-level F1 for GIF, the partition pair, and MEGU/IDEA
($|\mathrm{gap}|<2\%$ across all attack strategies), whereas
GNNDelete shows a much larger gap (mean ${\approx}11\%$ on Cora/GCN,
peaking at ${\sim}15\%$ on degree-based deletion), implicating
approximation error.

Hop-distance decay localizes this drift. On Cora/GCN ($L=2$), GIF and
the partition pair concentrate flips within the receptive field, while
GNNDelete remains closer to uniform across RF-relative buckets. MEGU and
IDEA stay near zero, matching their fingerprint positions. Per-family
curves and the raw 4-bucket breakdown are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:results}.

Update-detection AUC is not a proxy for utility vulnerability. MEGU
and IDEA combine near-null F1 attack effect with near-random deletion
visibility (AUC ${\approx}0.50$); the partition pair resists F1
collapse but shows the highest update visibility
(AUC $\approx 0.72$--$0.81$); and GNNDelete is moderately
exposed on both axes. We therefore treat this audit as a
complementary diagnostic, not as a calibrated cross-family privacy
ranking or as evidence for or against the F1 attack.
