\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We report five claim-conditioned slices: the 2D \emph{Vulnerability
Fingerprint}; its scale check on ogbn-arxiv; the architectural
\emph{Shard Protection Effect}; collateral damage via significance,
retrain gap, and hop-distance decay; and an MIA audit.

\subsection{Vulnerability Fingerprint}
\label{sec:results-fingerprint}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{figures/FIG-3_Spectrum.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Vulnerability Fingerprint} on the canonical cell
  (Cora/GCN/$r{=}0.05$, 5 seeds). Each point is a method-level cluster
  centre with a 1$\sigma$ ellipse over seeds; axes are the
  attack-specific F1 effects $\Delta F^{\text{attack}}_{\mathrm{IM}}$
  (structural-spread, $\alpha{=}0$) and
  $\Delta F^{\text{attack}}_{\mathrm{TracIn}}$ (gradient-signal,
  $\alpha{=}1$). Marker shape distinguishes pair members within each
  OpenGU category; chords visualise within-pair distance.
  \interim{Refresh with Phase~B numbers; current placeholder uses
  pre-Phase-B coordinates.}}
  \label{fig:fingerprint}
\end{figure}

The fingerprint plots the paired attack effect against the same-seed
random baseline, so its axes separate attack-specific signal from the
architectural term reported in \S\ref{sec:results-shard}. Because IM is
an L1 structural selector and TracIn is an L2-\textsc{direct}
model-coupled selector (Table~\ref{tab:access}), the plot also gives a
threat summary: the IM coordinate is a low-access floor, while the
TracIn coordinate is an upper bound on surrogate-model attacks.

Figure~\ref{fig:fingerprint} shows three regimes. \textbf{GNNDelete}
sits in the high-vulnerability quadrant
(\interim{$\Delta F^{\mathrm{attack}}_{\mathrm{IM}}{=}+16.9$},
\interim{$\Delta F^{\mathrm{attack}}_{\mathrm{TracIn}}{=}+X.X$};
$p$\interim{$<$0.001}, $N{=}5$). \textbf{GIF} is smaller and
TracIn-aligned (\interim{$+4.17\pm 0.76$}, $p$\interim{$=$0.0001}),
consistent with a Newton-step ceiling. \textbf{GraphEraser} and
\textbf{GraphRevoker} remain near the origin with non-positive attack
coordinates, while \textbf{MEGU} and \textbf{IDEA} stay close to zero
($<\interim{1\%}$ on both axes) despite belonging to vulnerable parent
families. This reveals a distinct \textbf{Family Pattern}: Learning-based 
methods are susceptible to severe, systemic global collapse (albeit with 
strong internal heterogeneity); IF-based methods (like GIF) are most 
vulnerable when attacked directly on their native gradient-signal axis; 
and Partition-based methods rely on physical sub-graph isolation for 
natural immunity against global interference (yielding Shard Protection). 
A 1D projection is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:results-1d}.

\subsection{The Shard Protection Effect}
\label{sec:results-shard}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/FIG-2_Scaling.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Architectural F1 shift $\Delta F_{\text{arch}}$ per
  method} (Cora/GCN, $r{=}0.05$). Bars below zero mark Shard
  Protection: the Partition pair's downstream F1 \emph{increases} when
  any small random set is unlearned. The fingerprint in
  \S\ref{sec:results-fingerprint} reports the orthogonal
  $\Delta F_{\text{attack}}$ term.
  \interim{Refresh with Phase~B numbers; current placeholder uses
  $k{=}5$ noise floor + budget-matched random.}}
  \label{fig:shard-protection}
\end{figure}

For the Partition family, the architectural term is negative:
$\Delta F_{\text{arch}}<0$, meaning random deletion at $r=0.05$
\emph{improves} F1 by \interim{$3$--$6\%$}. We call this
\textbf{Shard Protection}. Crucially, it is not an attack effect:
GraphEraser and GraphRevoker have $\Delta F_{\text{attack}}\approx 0$
in Figure~\ref{fig:fingerprint}, so adversarial selectors do not amplify
the gain beyond the random-deletion baseline.

The mechanism hypothesis is that partition aggregation localizes
unlearning to the affected shard while the remaining shard ensemble
absorbs the perturbation. We leave shard-purity and shard-count evidence
to Appendix~\ref{app:shard}; the main-text claim is the decomposition:
Shard Protection belongs to $\Delta F_{\text{arch}}$, not to the
attacker.

\subsection{Collateral Damage and Hop-Distance Decay}
\label{sec:results-hop}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{figures/FIG-4a_Significance.pdf}
  \hfill
  \includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{figures/FIG-4b_Effect.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Effect-size and significance heatmap} (5-seed paired
  $t$-test, Cora/GCN, $r{=}0.05$). Each cell is colored by the $p$-value
  of the one-sided test $\mu\le 0$. \interim{Refresh with Phase~B
  (decoupled IM seed) numbers; pre-fix data had Jaccard $=0.13$ between
  IM seeds.}}
  \label{fig:significance}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:significance} tests the paired effect over seeds. With
the \texttt{im\_selector\_seed} fix (\S\ref{app:imseed}), all but
\interim{one} GNNDelete IM/Hybrid cells clear $p<0.05$; GIF is
significant for TracIn but weaker for Hybrid; MEGU and IDEA show no
significant cell across the six strategies.

The retrain gap verifies that utility loss is not merely data loss:
retraining on $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{tr}}\setminus S$ recovers
baseline-level F1 for GIF and the partition pair (gap
\interim{$<$2\%}), whereas GNNDelete shows a much larger gap
(\interim{20.5\%}), implicating approximation error.

Hop-distance decay localizes this drift. On Cora/GCN ($L=2$), GIF and
the partition pair concentrate flips within the receptive field, while
GNNDelete remains closer to uniform across RF-relative buckets. MEGU and
IDEA stay near zero, matching their fingerprint positions. Per-family
curves and the raw 4-bucket breakdown are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:results}.

MIA AUC is not a proxy for utility vulnerability. MEGU combines near-null
F1 attack effect with lower membership leakage (AUC
\interim{$\approx 0.37$}); the partition pair resists F1 collapse but
retains weak-to-moderate leakage (AUC \interim{$\approx 0.60$}); and
GNNDelete is weak on both axes. We therefore treat MIA as a complementary
diagnostic, not as evidence for or against the F1 attack.
