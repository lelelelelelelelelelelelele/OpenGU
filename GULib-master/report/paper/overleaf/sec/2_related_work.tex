\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Graph unlearning and approximate updates.}
Graph unlearning asks for an already-trained GNN to remove the effect of
specified nodes, edges, or features without retraining from scratch.
Existing methods are commonly organized by the mechanism used to
approximate this update. \emph{Partition-based} methods follow the
SISA~\cite{bourtoule2021machine} idea: split the graph into shards, train
sub-models, and retrain only the affected shard after a deletion request
(e.g., GraphEraser~\cite{chen2022graph} and GraphRevoker).
\emph{Influence-function-based} methods estimate the parameter change
induced by deletion and apply a closed-form or proximal update
(e.g., GIF~\cite{wu2023gif}, CEU, CGU, GST, and certified variants such
as IDEA~\cite{dong2024idea}). \emph{Learning-based} methods instead train
deletion-aware objectives or auxiliary update modules
(e.g., GNNDelete~\cite{cheng2023gnndelete}, MEGU~\cite{li2024towards},
GUKD, D2DGN, and SGU). Projection- or deployment-time methods such as
Projector and UtU form a separate category and are not evaluated in this
paper. Existing evaluations of these methods largely use random or
protocol-driven deletion requests; our work treats the deletion set
itself as an adversarial choice and tests whether vulnerability is
structured by the underlying unlearning mechanism.

\paragraph{Adversarial deletion requests and unlearning red-teaming.}
Prior attacks on machine unlearning primarily study poisoning or
camouflaged requests in non-graph settings~\cite{marchant2022hard,di2022hidden},
where the attacker crafts samples or chooses deletions to slow down,
destabilize, or survive the unlearning procedure. Related red-team
evaluations of removal and safety mechanisms in vision models ask whether
nominally removed concepts can be recovered or bypassed~\cite{huang2024exploring}.
These lines motivate a broader view of unlearning as an exposed safety
surface. Graph unlearning adds a distinct failure mode: deleting one node
changes not only one training example but also the message-passing context
of its neighbors. As a result, legal deletion requests can have
non-local effects, and the central question becomes which deletion sets
amplify approximation error beyond the utility loss caused by data removal
alone.

\paragraph{Influence and diffusion primitives for deletion selection.}
Influence functions were originally introduced to estimate how individual
training examples affect model predictions~\cite{koh2017understanding}.
Graph unlearning methods such as GIF~\cite{wu2023gif} adapt this idea to
fast deletion updates, while TracIn~\cite{pruthi2020estimating} provides a
checkpoint-based influence proxy that avoids explicit Hessian inversion.
In parallel, influence maximization studies how to choose seed nodes that
maximize spread over a network~\cite{kempe2003maximizing}; CELF and
CELF++ accelerate the classical greedy objective~\cite{leskovec2007cost,goyal2011celf++}.
We use these two traditions only as selection primitives: influence scores
capture gradient-aligned sensitivity, while diffusion-style scores capture
structural spread. The access tiers, concrete scoring rules, and hybrid
selector are defined in \S\ref{sec:method}.
