\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Approximate graph unlearning.}
Existing GU methods fall into three mechanism families.
\emph{Shard-based} methods partition the graph into disjoint subgraphs, train
a sub-model per shard, and aggregate at inference;
deletion only retrains the affected shard
(GraphEraser~\cite{chen2022graph}, GUIDE, GraphRevoker).
\emph{Influence-function} methods estimate the leave-one-out parameter
delta in closed form and apply a single Newton or proximal step
(GIF~\cite{wu2023gif}, CEU, CGU, GST).
\emph{Learning-based} methods retrain selectively over a deletion-aware
objective (GNNDelete~\cite{cheng2023gnndelete}, GUKD, Projector, MEGU,
IDEA, ScaleGUN, D2DGN, SGU). All are evaluated under \emph{random} deletion
sets; we are unaware of prior work that audits them under adversarial
deletion-set selection.

\paragraph{Adversarial machine unlearning.}
The closest line of work studies poisoning of unlearning requests for
\emph{tabular} models~\cite{marchant2022hard,di2022hidden}, where the attacker
crafts samples or selects deletion sets to slow down or destabilize
retraining. Camouflaged data poisoning~\cite{di2022hidden} demonstrates that
hidden trigger injection can survive unlearning. None of these methods are
directly applicable to graph data, where structural dependence between
training points violates the i.i.d.\ assumptions underlying their analysis.
Concurrent vision-domain work~\cite{huang2024exploring} probes unlearning of
classifiers but does not consider graph structure.

\paragraph{Influence functions and influence maximization on graphs.}
Influence-function approximations for deep models~\cite{koh2017understanding}
have been extended to GNNs (GIF~\cite{wu2023gif}, GST). TracIn~\cite{pruthi2020estimating}
provides a checkpoint-based, gradient-only proxy that avoids the Hessian
inversion cost. Influence maximization (IM) on networks is a classical
problem~\cite{kempe2003maximizing}; CELF~\cite{leskovec2007cost} and
CELF++~\cite{goyal2011celf++} are submodular acceleration heuristics. We
adapt IM as an attack-side selector under independent-cascade simulation, with
a decoupled Monte-Carlo seed (\S\ref{sec:method}) for cross-seed
reproducibility.

\paragraph{Membership inference and model auditing.}
MIA against GNNs~\cite{olatunji2021membership,he2021stealing} provides
a complementary privacy lens. Our framework reports per-method MIA AUC as
a privacy-leakage diagnostic (\S\ref{sec:experiment}); the \emph{utility-collapse}
attacks we study are orthogonal to MIA but share the threat model of an
adversary with deletion-API access.
